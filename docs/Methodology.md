## Part II: Methodology Section (IEEE Format) — Guidance and Draft

### How to Frame This in an IEEE Paper

The IEEE format for a Data Science / ML conference paper follows a strict structure. The **Methodology** section (Section III in a 6-section paper) must communicate five things precisely:

1. **Dataset and experimental setup** — what data, what split, why that split
2. **Feature engineering rationale** — each feature group tied to a literature source
3. **Model selection and training protocol** — why these models, with what training procedure
4. **Evaluation framework** — which metrics, why, and their precise formulas
5. **Validity and reproducibility controls** — what you did to prevent leakage, ensure reproducibility

### Draft Methodology Section (IEEE Journal Format)

**III. METHODOLOGY**

**A. Dataset and Experimental Configuration**

This study uses the Corporación Favorita Grocery Sales Forecasting dataset [cite Kaggle], comprising approximately 3.0 million daily sales observations spanning January 2013 through August 2017, covering 54 stores and 33 product families. Six relational tables are joined on `(store_nbr, date)` composite keys: transaction records, store metadata, crude oil prices (WTI daily closing price, a documented economic driver for Ecuador [4]), holiday events, and daily foot traffic counts. The primary modelling unit is the `(store_nbr, family, date)` triple, yielding a panel regression problem with 1,782 distinct time series.

In accordance with the official competition evaluation protocol, the temporal holdout is fixed at August 16–31, 2017 (16 days). All model training uses data strictly preceding August 16, 2017; no shuffling of temporal records is performed at any stage. This constraint is enforced as a runtime invariant throughout the pipeline.

**B. Preprocessing and Data Integrity**

Approximately 44 missing values in the daily oil price series (arising from weekend and public holiday trading gaps) are imputed by forward-fill followed by backward-fill within the globally date-sorted series. Transaction counts exhibit approximately 2% missingness and are forward-filled within each store group.

Holiday events require careful treatment. The `holidays_events.csv` file contains rows with `transferred=True`, indicating days officially designated as holidays but observed on an alternative date. Following the roadmap specification, these records are excluded from all active-holiday binary flags; treating them as active holidays would introduce systematic feature errors on the affected dates. Zero-sale observations are retained without imputation and classified into three mutually exclusive categories following Svetunkov and Sroginis [10]: structural zeros (where a product family has zero mean sales across its entire history at a given store), event-driven zeros (arising from the April–May 2016 earthquake period, flagged as a binary `is_outlier` feature), and intermittent zeros (residual sparse demand). Outlier detection uses a per-`(store_nbr, family)` IQR criterion with a 3× IQR threshold; flagged rows are retained as training observations with an `is_outlier` covariate to preserve temporal continuity.

**C. Feature Engineering**

Feature construction follows nine groups, each grounded in prior literature. _Temporal features_ encode cyclical calendar structure (day-of-week, week-of-year, month, quarter, year, and a weekend indicator) consistent with the contextual augmentation framework of Fatima and Salam [4], which reports the largest accuracy gains when weekday indicators and holiday flags are added to historical sales records. _Lag features_ at 1-, 7-, 14-, and 365-day horizons are computed within each `(store_nbr, family)` group to capture short-term autocorrelation and annual seasonality. Arboleda-Florez and Castro-Zuluaga [13] and Al-Dulaimi et al. [17] both identify the 7-day lag as the single highest-importance predictor across SHAP analyses of grocery demand models, making its inclusion non-negotiable.

_Rolling statistics_ (7-, 14-, and 28-day rolling mean and standard deviation) are constructed using the mandatory `.shift(1).rolling(w)` pattern rather than `.rolling(w)` directly. This distinction is critical: Cerqueira et al. [14] demonstrate that rolling features constructed without a prior shift allow the current target value to contaminate its own rolling mean, inflating apparent cross-validation performance by up to 20.5%.

_Promotional features_ include the raw `onpromotion` item count (a numerical covariate, not a binary flag), a 1-day lag, a 7-day rolling mean, and a continuous `promo_duration_rolling` feature encoding the count of consecutive promotion days in the preceding 14-day window. Al-Dulaimi et al. [17] demonstrate that promotions lasting fewer than three days yield negligible incremental lift, establishing the necessity of duration-aware encoding over simple binary flags. Hewage et al. [5] further show that incorporating `days_since_last_promotion` as a proximity feature reduces RMSE by 18% over spike-only promotion models by capturing the full demand lifecycle (pre-stockpiling, in-promotion uplift, and post-promotion demand dip).

_Macroeconomic features_ include the WTI crude oil price, a 7-day lag, and a 28-day rolling mean, motivated by the documented sensitivity of Ecuadorian retail sales to oil price fluctuations [4]. _Transaction volume_ (daily store foot traffic) is included with a 7-day lag as a leading indicator of future sales [4]. _Store metadata_ includes the store type (label-encoded A–E), cluster (integer 1–17), and target-encoded city and state — critically, target encoding is fit exclusively on training-partition rows to prevent test-set statistics from influencing training normalization [14].

_Cannibalization proxy features_ append, for each `(store, family, date)`, the 7-day lagged mean sales of the top-3 most negatively correlated product families during promotion-active periods in the same store. The cross-family correlation map is derived exclusively from the training partition.

Feature rows lacking valid 365-day lag values (the first calendar year per series) are dropped before model training, removing approximately one year of data per group. This is expected and correct: the resulting training window spans over four years per series.

**D. Model Development**

Four models are trained and evaluated in increasing order of complexity. _Baseline 1: Seasonal Naïve_ predicts each day's sales as the value observed 365 days prior within the same `(store, family)` group, establishing the performance floor below which no model can be considered useful [3]. _Baseline 2: Gradient Descent Linear Regression_ is implemented from scratch using NumPy, with L1 and L2 regularisation applied analytically within the gradient update. The target variable is log1p-transformed prior to training to address right-skew and zero-inflation [8]; predictions are recovered via inverse transform (expm1). Convergence is monitored over 1,000 iterations and the loss trajectory is plotted for validation.

_Advanced Model 1: XGBoost Regressor_ is trained with the `hist` tree method, n*estimators=500, and early stopping at 50 rounds. \_Advanced Model 2: LightGBM Regressor* uses 63 leaves, minimum child samples of 20, and feature fraction 0.8. Both advanced models are first trained with default parameters, then tuned via Bayesian optimisation using the Optuna framework with the TPE sampler [15]. Ozaki et al. [15] confirm that the TPE sampler identifies near-optimal configurations for tabular datasets of this scale (approximately 3 million rows) in 3–5× fewer trials than grid search. Hyperparameter search spaces follow the functional requirements: n_estimators (200–1,000), max_depth (3–10), learning_rate (0.01–0.3, log-uniform), subsample (0.5–1.0), and colsample_bytree (0.5–1.0) for XGBoost; and num_leaves (20–150), max_depth (3–12), learning_rate, min_child_samples (5–50), and feature_fraction (0.5–1.0) for LightGBM.

Cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning uses `TimeSeriesSplit(n_splits=5)`, enforcing strict temporal ordering. Standard K-fold cross-validation with shuffling is explicitly rejected: Cerqueira et al. [14] show that constructing train/validation sequences before the temporal split allows future information to contaminate training, with leakage-induced RMSE inflation reaching 20.5% under standard 10-fold CV compared to clean temporal splits. All StandardScaler instances are fit on the training partition only, then applied to both training and test data, preventing test-set statistics from influencing normalisation.

**E. Evaluation Framework**

All models are evaluated on the held-out 16-day test window (August 16–31, 2017) using four metrics. The primary metric is RMSLE:

$$\text{RMSLE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\log(1+\hat{y}_i) - \log(1+y_i))^2}$$

RMSLE is preferred over RMSE as the primary metric because the target `sales` is right-skewed, contains structural zeros, and retail stockout cost exceeds overstock cost — all three properties favour RMSLE's logarithmic compression and asymmetric under-prediction penalty [8]. MAPE is reported but excludes zero-actual rows (where it is undefined) and is treated as a secondary metric. R² and RMSE complete the reporting table.

Residual analysis is performed for each model: the mean residual and its ratio to mean actual sales are computed. A mean residual exceeding 5% of mean actual sales is treated as evidence of systematic bias warranting explicit documentation.

**F. Cannibalization and Promotional Impact Analysis**

Cross-product cannibalization is identified using the methodology specified in FR-08. A cross-family correlation matrix is computed on residual sales (actual minus rolling 28-day mean, net of trend) during `onpromotion > 0` periods. Product family pairs with Pearson r < −0.35 during promotion windows are classified as cannibalization candidates; statistical significance is reported via p-values. The expected candidates — BEVERAGES vs LIQUOR/WINE/BEER — are consistent with the cross-family correlation structure identified in exploratory analysis.

Promotional lift for each distinct markdown event is quantified as:

$$\text{lift} = \frac{s_{\text{actual}} - \hat{s}_{\text{counterfactual}}}{\hat{s}_{\text{counterfactual}}}$$

where $\hat{s}_{\text{counterfactual}}$ is the rolling 4-week pre-promotion mean sales, following the specification in FR-08. Lift is computed for a minimum of 10 distinct promotion events per the acceptance criteria.

**G. Interpretability via SHAP Analysis**

SHAP TreeExplainer values are computed for the best-performing tree model. Global feature importance is visualised as a mean absolute SHAP value bar chart [13]. Two validation criteria are enforced: at least one promotional feature and at least one temporal/lag feature must appear in the top-5 SHAP contributors, consistent with the convergent findings of Arboleda-Florez and Castro-Zuluaga [13] and Al-Dulaimi et al. [17] in independent grocery demand studies. Dependence plots are generated to reveal interaction effects, particularly the promotion × weekday interaction documented by Arboleda-Florez and Castro-Zuluaga [13].

**H. Reproducibility Controls**

All models use `random_state=42`. `PYTHONHASHSEED` is set to 42 at process start. Package versions are pinned in `requirements.txt`. SHA-256 hashes of all input CSV files are logged to `data/checksums.txt`. The best-performing model is saved via joblib and immediately reloaded; bit-for-bit prediction identity between the original and reloaded model is verified programmatically as a post-serialisation invariant.

## Part III: Additional Pointers on Conveyable Project Methodology

### The Overarching Narrative to Convey

Your paper's methodology must communicate **one coherent story**: you took a standard retail forecasting problem and elevated it from a regression exercise into an interpretable, business-grounded analysis by making every design decision traceable to first principles. Here is how to frame each subsection:

**1. Why a temporal holdout instead of random split?** — This is your first opportunity to demonstrate methodological maturity. Cite [14] and [24] explicitly. Reviewers know that random splits on time series are wrong; demonstrating that you know _why_ (future data contaminates lag features during cross-validation) signals engineering rigour.

**2. Why RMSLE over RMSE or accuracy?** — This is a common beginner mistake in regression tasks. Your paper should state the _economic_ justification (stockout cost asymmetry) from [8], not just the mathematical convenience.

**3. Why retain zeros instead of removing them?** — This is the most nuanced preprocessing decision. Reference [9] and [10] explicitly. The three-category zero taxonomy (structural/event-driven/intermittent) is what separates a data-science practitioner from a data-science student.

**4. Why XGBoost + LightGBM over a neural network?** — Reference [3] and [22] to justify. The 16-day short horizon is a critical mediating factor: [3] shows that deep learning accuracy gains over gradient boosting narrow substantially on horizons ≤ 2 weeks.

**5. How does cannibalization analysis elevate the work?** — Frame this as the _novel contribution_ section. Most forecasting papers stop at model comparison. Your work extends to causal business insight: which promotions create genuine demand versus merely shifting it between categories. This is the section the examiner will remember.

**6. What to put in Future Work?** — Chronos-2 [23] and TimeGPT [22] as zero-shot baselines; Switch-Hurdle [11] for intermittent families; robust hierarchical reconciliation [26] for multi-level coherence; probabilistic extensions [2] for inventory-facing quantile forecasts; weekly retraining cadence [29] for production deployment.

### IEEE Formatting Checklist

- Every equation must be numbered: `(1)`, `(2)`, etc.
- Every table must have a caption above it (IEEE style)
- Every figure must have a caption below it
- Citations are numbered in order of first appearance: `[1]`, `[2]`, etc.
- The methodology section should be approximately 800–1,200 words in a 6-page IEEE conference format, or up to 2,000 words in a journal format
- Do NOT describe software libraries in the methodology (pandas, sklearn belong in an Implementation Details section or a footnote, not the main methodology)
- Use passive voice for experimental procedures: "models were trained", "features were constructed" — this is standard IEEE scientific style
- Equations should be stated, then referenced in prose: "...computed using (1), where ŷᵢ denotes..."
